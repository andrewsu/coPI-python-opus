# Luke Wiseman Lab — Public Profile

**PI:** Luke Wiseman
**Institution:** The Scripps Research Institute
**Department:** Department of Molecular Medicine

## Research Summary

The Wiseman lab at The Scripps Research Institute investigates the molecular mechanisms by which stress-responsive signaling pathways regulate proteostasis across cellular compartments, with particular emphasis on the unfolded protein response (UPR) and its impact on secretory and extracellular proteostasis. A central theme of the lab's work is defining how pharmacologic activation of the ATF6 arm of the UPR can reduce secretion and toxic aggregation of destabilized amyloidogenic proteins, including transthyretin (TTR) and immunoglobulin light chains implicated in systemic amyloid diseases. Using quantitative interactomics, the lab has revealed that ATF6 activation promotes ER retention of amyloidogenic proteins through increased interactions with BiP, PDIA4, and other ER proteostasis factors. The lab has further demonstrated that pharmacologic ATF6 activation confers broad tissue protection in ischemia/reperfusion injury models across heart, kidney, and brain. In parallel, the Wiseman lab studies mitochondrial quality control, elucidating the structures and regulatory mechanisms of AAA+ proteases AFG3L2 and YME1L, and how the PERK arm of the UPR coordinates mitochondrial morphology and function during ER stress. Additional work has uncovered non-canonical roles for IRE1α at mitochondria-associated ER membranes in calcium signaling and bioenergetics. More recently, the lab has explored integrated stress response activation through RIG-I/HRI signaling, broadening their pharmacologic toolkit for modulating stress-responsive proteostasis pathways in neurodegenerative and amyloid diseases.

## Key Methods and Technologies

- quantitative interactome proteomics (mass spectrometry-based interactomics)
- cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure determination
- targeted RNA sequencing for stress-responsive pathway deconvolution
- RNA-Seq transcriptional profiling
- high-throughput cell-based reporter screening (ERSE-FLuc, XBP1s-RLuc)
- site-directed mutagenesis and structure-function analysis
- fluorogenic small molecule probe design and live-cell imaging
- induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC) derivation and disease modeling
- in vivo mouse models of ischemia/reperfusion (cardiac, renal, cerebral)
- C. elegans transgenic models for proteostasis studies
- co-immunoprecipitation and interactome analysis
- conformational reporter assays for secreted amyloidogenic proteins
- pharmacologic compound characterization (LC-MS purity, metabolic activation profiling)
- quantitative proteomics for transcription factor target gene set definition
- calcium transfer and bioenergetics assays at mitochondria-associated membranes

## Model Systems

- HEK293T and HEK293T-Rex cells with UPR reporter systems
- HEK293 DAX cells
- HepG2 cells and HepG2 ATF6 cells
- ATF6+/+ and ATF6-/- mouse embryonic fibroblasts
- cardiac myocyte-specific Atf6 knockout mice
- IRE1α conditional knockout mice (liver-specific)
- C. elegans expressing human TTR transgenically
- ATTR amyloidosis patient-derived iPSC lines
- FLAG-tagged TTR variant expression systems (wild-type and destabilized mutants)
- destabilized immunoglobulin light chain (LC) expression systems for AL amyloidosis modeling
- mouse models of myocardial, renal, and cerebral ischemia/reperfusion injury
- yeast YME1 and human AFG3L2 reconstituted systems for structural studies

## Disease Areas / Biological Processes

- transthyretin (TTR) amyloidosis (ATTR amyloidosis)
- light chain amyloidosis (AL amyloidosis)
- systemic amyloid diseases
- ischemia/reperfusion injury (myocardial infarction, stroke, renal ischemia)
- neurodegenerative diseases (spinocerebellar ataxia, neurodegeneration linked to AFG3L2 mutations)
- achromatopsia (ATF6-linked cone photoreceptor disease)
- ER stress-associated protein misfolding diseases
- mitochondrial dysfunction and mitochondrial protease-linked disorders
- age-related proteostasis decline
